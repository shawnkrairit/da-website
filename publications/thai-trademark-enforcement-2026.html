Trademark Enforcement Strategies in Thailand: A Practitioner's Perspective

By Prof. Dej-Udom Krairit March 2026 12 min read
Listen to this publication
Estimated 12 min

Introduction: Thailand's Intellectual Property Landscape

Thailand has made significant strides in strengthening its intellectual property protection framework over the past two decades. As a member of the World Trade Organisation and a signatory to the Paris Convention, the country has progressively aligned its IP legislation with international standards. For trademark owners, this evolution has created a more robust environment for protecting and enforcing brand rights, though practical challenges remain that require careful strategic planning.

The Thai trademark system operates under the Trademark Act B.E. 2534 (1991), as amended, which provides the statutory foundation for registration, opposition, and enforcement. Thailand follows a first-to-file system, making early registration essential for any brand owner seeking to operate in the Thai market. However, registration alone is insufficient. Effective trademark protection requires a comprehensive enforcement strategy that leverages the full range of available legal mechanisms.

Administrative Enforcement Through the DIP

The Department of Intellectual Property (DIP), operating under the Ministry of Commerce, serves as the primary administrative authority for trademark matters in Thailand. Trademark owners may file complaints with the DIP requesting investigation of suspected infringement. The DIP has the authority to conduct preliminary investigations and, where warranted, refer matters to the Royal Thai Police or the Department of Special Investigation for further action.

Administrative complaints to the DIP are particularly useful as an initial step in enforcement campaigns. They create an official record of the complaint, may prompt voluntary cessation of infringing activity, and can serve as a foundation for subsequent criminal or civil proceedings. The DIP also operates a mediation service that can facilitate resolution of trademark disputes without the need for formal litigation, which can be an efficient and cost-effective approach for certain categories of infringement.

Civil Litigation in the IP&IT Court

Thailand's Central Intellectual Property and International Trade Court (IP&IT Court), established in 1997, is a specialised court with jurisdiction over intellectual property disputes. The court has developed considerable expertise in trademark cases and offers a number of procedural advantages for rights holders, including the ability to grant preliminary injunctions and the availability of both damages and account of profits as remedies.

Effective trademark enforcement in Thailand requires not a single action but a coordinated strategy that deploys administrative, civil, and criminal mechanisms in concert, calibrated to the specific nature and scale of the infringement encountered.

Civil proceedings in the IP&IT Court typically proceed through pleadings, witness examination, and judgment. The court has shown an increasing willingness to award meaningful damages in trademark cases, particularly where the rights holder can demonstrate actual loss or the infringer's profits. In addition, the court may issue permanent injunctions prohibiting further infringing conduct and order the destruction of infringing goods. For international trademark owners, the IP&IT Court's familiarity with cross-border issues and international IP norms makes it a preferred forum for complex enforcement matters.

Criminal Enforcement

Criminal enforcement remains a significant component of Thailand's trademark protection framework. Under the Trademark Act, the manufacture, import, sale, or offering for sale of goods bearing a counterfeit trademark is a criminal offence punishable by imprisonment of up to four years and fines of up to 400,000 baht. The Economic Crime Suppression Division (ECD) of the Royal Thai Police is the primary agency responsible for investigating and prosecuting trademark counterfeiting cases.

Criminal enforcement is particularly effective against large-scale counterfeiting operations, market vendors selling counterfeit goods, and cases where the infringer is a repeat offender. The threat of criminal prosecution and imprisonment serves as a powerful deterrent. In practice, criminal enforcement actions often begin with a police raid on premises suspected of manufacturing or storing counterfeit goods, following which the matter proceeds through the criminal courts. Rights holders should ensure that their evidence package is well-prepared before initiating a criminal complaint, as the burden of proof in criminal proceedings is higher than in civil matters.

Customs Recordal and Border Measures

Thailand's Customs Act provides for the recordal of trademarks with the Thai Customs Department, enabling customs officers to detain shipments of suspected counterfeit goods at the border. This is a critically important tool for trademark owners, as it allows enforcement action before infringing goods enter the domestic market. Once a trademark is recorded, customs officers who identify suspicious shipments will notify the rights holder, who then has a specified period to confirm the goods are counterfeit and take appropriate legal action.

The customs recordal system has proven effective in intercepting counterfeit goods arriving by sea and air, and Thailand's position as a major transit hub for goods in the ASEAN region makes border enforcement particularly important. Rights holders should ensure that their customs recordals are kept current and that they maintain close communication with customs officials, providing regular training materials and updated product information to assist in the identification of counterfeit goods.

Online Enforcement Challenges

The growth of e-commerce and social media platforms has created new challenges for trademark enforcement in Thailand. Counterfeit goods are increasingly sold through online marketplaces, social media channels, and messaging applications, making detection and enforcement more complex. While Thailand's Computer Crime Act and the recently updated Electronic Commerce Act provide some legal tools for addressing online infringement, the practical challenges of identifying anonymous sellers and obtaining takedown orders remain significant.

Major e-commerce platforms operating in Thailand have implemented brand protection programmes that allow trademark owners to report infringing listings for removal. These programmes, while not a substitute for legal enforcement, provide a practical first line of defence against online counterfeiting. Rights holders should establish relationships with the brand protection teams of major platforms and implement regular monitoring programmes to detect and respond to infringing listings promptly.

Practical Recommendations

Effective trademark enforcement in Thailand requires a multi-faceted approach that combines proactive protection measures with responsive enforcement actions. Based on our experience advising clients across a wide range of industries, we recommend that trademark owners consider the following key elements in their enforcement strategy:

  • Comprehensive registration: Ensure that all relevant marks are registered in Thailand, covering the full range of goods and services offered in the market.
  • Customs recordal: Record trademarks with the Thai Customs Department to enable border enforcement and interception of counterfeit shipments.
  • Regular market monitoring: Implement systematic monitoring of both physical and online markets to identify infringing activity early.
  • Coordinated enforcement: Deploy administrative, civil, and criminal mechanisms in a coordinated manner, calibrated to the nature and scale of the infringement.
  • Evidence preservation: Maintain thorough records of all infringing activity and enforcement actions, as this documentation is essential for building strong cases in any forum.
  • Industry collaboration: Participate in industry associations and rights holder coalitions that coordinate enforcement efforts and share intelligence on counterfeiting trends.
